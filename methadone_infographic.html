Mechanism of Action & Pharmacokinetics

μ-Opioid Receptor Agonist

Analgesia, respiratory depression, decreased GI motility. Primary analgesic mechanism.

NMDA Receptor Antagonist

Prevents opioid tolerance & hyperalgesia. Synergistic with ketamine. Extends analgesia beyond plasma levels.

SNRI Activity

Serotonin & norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor. Modulates central pain pathways. Also has δ and κ opioid receptor affinity.

🌟 What makes it unique: The ONLY perioperative opioid combining all three mechanisms. Analgesia outlasts measurable plasma concentration — non-opioid mechanisms contribute beyond the elimination phase.

Administration & Anesthetic Adjustments

💉Route & Timing
  • IV (General Surgery): Slow bolus over ~1 min
  • IV (Cardiac / High-Risk): Slow infusion over 30 minutes preferred
  • Oral: Administer at least 30–60 min before incision
  • Timing: Give at or shortly after induction — peak respiratory depression occurs while patient is intubated ✓
  • Do NOT re-dose intraoperatively in opioid-naïve patients
  • Reduce or eliminate other intraoperative opioids — small supplemental fentanyl (100–200 mcg) acceptable for intubation
🫀Hemodynamic & Safety Profile
  • No histamine release — no red man syndrome or flushing
  • No unique hemodynamic effects at standard doses
  • Monitor BP as with any opioid
  • No special cardiac monitoring required for a single intraoperative dose
  • QTc effects associated with chronic use only (not single perioperative dose)

Patient Selection Criteria & Contraindications

✅ Ideal Candidates
  • Opioid-naïve adults undergoing moderate–severe pain surgeries: spine (multilevel fusion), cardiac (CABG, valve), open abdominal, thoracic, gynecologic, CRS/HIPEC
  • Case duration ≥1–2 hours (allows peak respiratory depression to resolve before extubation)
  • Patients at risk for chronic post-surgical pain (NMDA antagonism may reduce central sensitization)
  • Bariatric patients — reduces total opioid burden in high-risk respiratory population
  • Renal failure / ESRD — safe, does not accumulate
  • Same-day and next-day discharge surgery at appropriate doses (0.1–0.25 mg/kg IBW)
✗ Absolute Contraindications
  • Known anaphylaxis to methadone
  • Pre-existing respiratory depression or severe uncontrolled respiratory disease
  • Active ileus
  • QTc ≥500 ms
⚠️ Use with Caution
  • Multiple QT-prolonging medications (obtain preop ECG if risk factors present)
  • Serotonergic medications (SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, tramadol) — theoretical serotonin syndrome risk
  • Severe liver disease — start lower; CYP metabolism may be impaired
  • Very short cases (<45–60 min) — peak respiratory depression may overlap with extubation
  • Patients on CYP2B6 inducers/inhibitors
  • Elderly patients (>60 y) — slower elimination; reduce to 15 mg
🔄Patients on Chronic Methadone (OUD or Chronic Pain)
  • Continue home methadone — do not convert to another opioid
  • Verify dose with patient's methadone clinic preoperatively; document in EMR
  • Oral route preferred; if oral not possible: IV = 1/4 of daily oral dose BID (may titrate to QID)
  • Do NOT attempt opioid rotation — unreliable due to significant PK/PD variability
  • Expect higher opioid requirements than opioid-naïve patients
  • On discharge: arrange methadone clinic continuity
Managing Acute Post-Surgical Pain — Stepwise
1
Maximize multimodal non-opioid analgesics (acetaminophen, NSAIDs, ketamine, local anesthetics, steroids, gabapentinoids)
2
Add regional anesthetic techniques where feasible
3
Supplement with short-acting opioids as needed — expect higher requirements
✗
Avoid partial agonists (buprenorphine, butorphanol) — will precipitate withdrawal

PACU, Discharge & Pharmacy

🏥What PACU Needs to Know
  • Patient received intraoperative methadone — analgesic effect lasts up to 24–36 hours
  • Expect reduced opioid requirements — titrate rescue doses cautiously
  • Ensure RR ≥10 before giving additional opioids
  • Peak respiratory depression has already passed intraoperatively — ongoing vigilance still required
  • Extended monitoring warranted for obese patients, OSA, or elderly
  • Standard opioid discharge criteria apply — no unique methadone-specific PACU checklist needed for single intraoperative dose
🚨
If respiratory depression suspected: Do NOT give single-dose naloxone — start naloxone infusion ≥24 hours
🏠Patient Discharge Instructions
  • Pain relief may be prolonged up to 24 hours — expected and intentional
  • Avoid alcohol and CNS depressants for 24 hours
  • Do not drive or operate heavy machinery for 24–36 hours
  • No dietary restrictions specific to methadone
  • Patient may need fewer opioid pills at home — consider prescribing less; instruct on safe storage and disposal
💰Pharmacy & Cost Considerations

Methadone for pain (not addiction treatment) is a Schedule II opioid — most hospital pharmacies can stock without special licensing. To add to formulary: work with pharmacy director/formulary committee.

  • Shorter hospital LOS in select procedures
  • Fewer opioids prescribed at discharge
  • Possible reduction in chronic post-surgical pain → long-term downstream savings

📖Seminal Studies
Gourlay et al. (1982 & 1984)
Single 20 mg IV bolus at induction → median analgesic duration 27 hours. Of 23 patients: 39% required no postoperative analgesia, 26% required only non-opioids, 35% required opioid rescue. No patient required naloxone. Established MEAC (~30 ng/mL) and respiratory depression threshold (~100 ng/mL).
Wong et al. (2026) — Most Recent RCT
80-patient cardiac surgery RCT. Methadone 0.2 mg/kg vs. morphine 0.2 mg/kg. Results: 63% less morphine at 24h, 69% less overall; lower pain scores through 72h; higher satisfaction at all time points; no difference in adverse effects. Analgesia persisted 48–72h post-extubation after levels fell below MEAC — attributed to NMDA and SNRI mechanisms.
